The Best Jewelry & Watch Collecting Educators on YouTube and Instagram

Jewelry and watch collecting is one of the most diverse and high-value collecting categories — spanning antique and estate jewelry, signed costume jewelry, vintage mechanical watches, luxury Swiss timepieces, and gemstone collecting. Unlike sports cards or coins, there is no centralized grading authority, making the expertise of trusted educators especially critical for new collectors.

The creators listed here cover the full spectrum: identifying hallmarks and maker's marks on antique jewelry, reading watch movements and understanding complications, evaluating gemstone quality without lab reports, navigating major auction houses and estate sales, and building a focused collection with long-term appreciation potential. Whether you collect Art Deco diamond brooches, signed Miriam Haskell costume pieces, vintage Rolex Submariners, or Georgian mourning jewelry — this directory has the right educators for you.

How to Choose the Right Jewelry & Watch Educator

The most effective jewelry and watch educators share a common trait: deep category specialization rather than broad general content. A creator who focuses exclusively on Art Deco platinum jewelry will teach you infinitely more about buying, identifying, and valuing pieces in that niche than a general lifestyle channel that covers "all things vintage." Look for creators who regularly show their own purchases, explain what they paid, and revisit pieces to update on value changes.

For watches specifically, prioritize creators who cover the secondary market mechanics — how to read auction results, where the value lies between dial variants and reference numbers, and how service history affects pricing. For jewelry, look for creators with gemological credentials (GIA Graduate Gemologist or equivalent) or demonstrable field experience buying from auction houses and estate sales rather than retail.

Frequently Asked Questions — Jewelry Collecting
The top jewelry collecting YouTubers include Antique Jewelry University, Estate Jewelry Investor, Lisa Severy Jewelry, Worthy.com's channel, and WP Diamonds. These channels cover estate jewelry buying, gem identification, hallmark reading, vintage jewelry eras, and jewelry investing strategy for both beginners and advanced collectors.
For dedicated watch collecting influencers, visit our Watches & Horology Hub → which covers Watchfinder & Co, Teddy Baldassarre, Hodinkee, Bark and Jack, RJ Watches, and 20+ more verified watch creators.
Antique jewelry identification involves reading hallmarks and maker's marks (use Antique Jewelry University's free online database), identifying the jewelry period (Georgian, Victorian, Edwardian, Art Nouveau, Art Deco), examining construction techniques (hand-fabricated vs. cast settings), and evaluating gemstone cuts characteristic of each era — rose cut, old mine cut, and old European cut all predate the modern brilliant cut and are strong period indicators.
For a deep dive on what makes a watch valuable, visit our Watches Hub. In brief: brand heritage, reference number, dial originality, movement condition, service history, and original papers all matter significantly.
The most collected jewelry periods are: Georgian (1714–1837) for hairwork, paste stones, and foil-backed gems; Victorian (1837–1901) for mourning jewelry, Scottish pebble pieces, and rose-cut diamonds; Edwardian (1900–1915) for platinum lacework and delicate millegrain settings; Art Nouveau (1890–1910) for enamel and nature motifs by makers like René Lalique; Art Deco (1920–1940) for geometric platinum and diamond pieces from Cartier and Van Cleef; and Mid-Century Modern (1940–1965) for bold gold designs from David Webb and Verdura.
For insurance appraisals, seek a GIA Graduate Gemologist (GG) or an appraiser certified by the American Society of Jewelry Appraisers (ASJA) or American Gem Society (AGS). Appraisals should be updated every 3–5 years as market values fluctuate significantly. Expect to pay $50–$150+ per item. Never use a jeweler who offers a free appraisal with the intent to buy the piece — seek a fee-only independent appraiser who has no financial interest in the outcome.
See our dedicated Watches & Horology Hub → for a complete guide to buying vintage watches, including the best dealers, auction houses, and what to look for in Rolex, Omega, and Patek Philippe.
The most collectible signed jewelry houses include Cartier, Van Cleef & Arpels, Bulgari, Tiffany & Co. (especially early pieces signed Tiffany & Co. before 1900), David Webb, Jean Schlumberger, Suzanne Belperron, and Verdura. In costume jewelry, Miriam Haskell, Chanel, Christian Dior, and Schiaparelli are highly sought-after. Signed pieces consistently command 200–500% premiums over unsigned equivalent unsigned pieces in gold, diamond, or gemstone content — the maker's name is often worth more than the materials themselves.
